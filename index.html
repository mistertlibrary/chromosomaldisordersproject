Source Quality

For a science project at this level, your sources should come from peer-reviewed journals, government health agencies (NIH, CDC), university research programs, or established medical references. Wikipedia is useful for initial orientation; it is not appropriate for citation. When you encounter an unfamiliar source, use the Research Skills tab to evaluate it before building an argument atop it.

Find Sources

Search PubMed

PubMed indexes millions of biomedical and life sciences journal articles, many freely available through PubMed Central. Add your disorder's name to find peer-reviewed research.

Search OMIM

Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man is a comprehensive, authoritative catalog of genetic disorders and their molecular basis. Excellent for the Genetic Cause section.

Library Databases

Database Science Reference Source

EBSCO's science database combining encyclopedic reference with periodical coverage. Strong for all project sections. Includes APA citation export tools.

eBooks Science Reference Source eBooks

Companion eBook collection. Useful when you need sustained reading on a condition rather than article-level snippets. Good for rarer disorders with limited article coverage.

Database Science Online

Infobase reference database. Check here if other databases have limited coverage of your specific condition — particularly useful for rarer disorders on the project list.

Multi-disciplinary Academic Search Complete

The broadest academic database in your library. Covers journals, magazines, and books across all disciplines. Use for peer-reviewed science articles and for ethical or social perspectives.

Ebooks MAS Ultra: School Edition

Designed for high school libraries, MAS Reference eBook Collection is a full-text reference database of ebooks covering many subjects, including history, science and health. It also includes thousands of full-text primary source documents and videos from the Associated Press.

Search Strategy Tips

  • Use Boolean operators to combine or narrow terms: Klinefelter syndrome AND testosterone or trisomy 21 AND maternal age
  • Use quotation marks for exact phrases: "nondisjunction meiosis II" returns only results with that exact string
  • In PubMed, look for MeSH terms. These are standardized vocabulary terms that find related articles even when researchers use different language for the same concept
  • Trace citations: once you find a strong article, check its reference list and look up who has cited it since publication. This is how researchers find related work and assess a study's influence

Understand the Biology

Reference Sources

NIH Reference MedlinePlus Genetics

National Library of Medicine's genetics portal. Clear, accurate summaries of hundreds of conditions. An excellent starting point — treat as background reading and orientation, not a primary research source.

Citable Summary GeneReviews (NCBI / Univ. of Washington)

Expert-authored, peer-reviewed summaries of genetic conditions, hosted on NCBI and maintained by the University of Washington. Note that GeneReviews focuses primarily on single-gene disorders — not all chromosomal conditions on this project's list will have a GeneReviews entry. Where an entry exists, it is among the most rigorous citable sources available for your Genetic Cause and Biological Impact sections.

Reference Gale Encyclopedia of Genetic Disorders

Structured entries covering description, diagnosis, demographics, treatment, and genetic profile. When accessed through a Gale database, the citation export tool generates citations in APA, MLA, and Chicago formats — use the APA export for this project. A strong starting reference for any disorder on the list.

NIH National Human Genome Research Institute

Authoritative fact sheets and glossary from the institute that led the Human Genome Project. Strong for chromosome structure, genomic terminology, and visual diagrams.

Database OMIM

Comprehensive catalog of genetic disorders and genes. Dense and technical — best consulted after you have foundational understanding. Essential for accurate detail on chromosomal specifics and gene involvement.

Background Your Genome

Produced by Wellcome Connecting Science, the Wellcome Sanger Institute's learning, training and engagement programme, based on the Wellcome Genome Campus near Cambridge, UK. Good for background reading on chromosomes and genetic concepts before you engage with technical literature.

Introductory level — use for orientation, not as a primary citation.

Introductory level — use for concept review, not as a research source.

Data & Statistics

What Makes a Strong Data Source?

Your data should come from the organization that collected it, not a secondary site that summarized it. Government health agencies, academic research programs, and international disease registries are your strongest options. When you write your 3–5 sentence interpretation, explain not just what the data shows, but why it is biologically significant! Connect the numbers back to the mechanisms you described in your Genetic Cause and Biological Impact sections.

Data Sources

CDC CDC — Birth Defects Data & Statistics

The CDC's National Center on Birth Defects and Developmental Disabilities (NCBDDD) publishes U.S. prevalence data for chromosomal conditions. Strong for trisomies and sex chromosome aneuploidies. Downloadable data tables.

International Orphanet

An international rare disease knowledge base established in France by INSERM in 1997 and now coordinated across a consortium of 40 countries worldwide. Contains detailed epidemiology and prevalence data, a peer-reviewed encyclopedia of rare diseases, and directories of expert resources. Particularly strong for rarer conditions on the project list.

Registry EUROCAT

European network of population-based registries for congenital anomaly surveillance, covering approximately 25% of European births annually. Aggregate prevalence data and pan-European trend reports are publicly available. Note that individual registry-level data is now restricted under GDPR — use aggregate reports and published statistics for your data visualizations.

NIH NIH Genetic and Rare Diseases Information Center (GARD)

Managed by the National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences (NCATS) at NIH. Provides plain-language summaries of rare diseases, links to clinical studies, patient registries, and curated statistics for many conditions on the project list. Available in English and Spanish.

Chart & Graph Tools

Free Tool Datawrapper

Browser-based chart builder. You can start creating immediately without an account, but a free account is required to save and return to your work. Input CDC or Orphanet data and produce clean, labeled charts. Well-suited for prevalence rate graphs and population statistics tables.

Free Tool Flourish

Similar to Datawrapper with additional chart types. Particularly well-suited for maternal age vs. risk graphs. Free tier supports all chart types needed for this project.

Citing Your Data and Figures in APA

Whether you created it or reproduced it, every graph, chart, or table must be cited. See the Research Skills tab for APA 7th edition figure citation formats. If you re-visualized data from a primary source using Datawrapper or Flourish, cite the original data source in your References page, not the tool you used to make the chart.

Ethics & Society

Moving Beyond Surface-Level Analysis

The ethics section is where many projects remain thin, with students naming an issue without actually analyzing it. Strong responses identify a specific ethical tension (not just a topic), describe competing perspectives on it, and connect the issue back to the biological realities you've already described. The guiding questions below can help you build that kind of analysis.

Debate & Perspectives Databases

A Note on How to Use These Resources

These databases are designed to present structured competing perspectives. They are excellent for identifying and framing an ethical question and understanding the arguments on multiple sides. They are not peer-reviewed research sources. Use them to sharpen your analysis and locate the real tension in your chosen issue; support that analysis with primary literature from the Find Sources and Ethics resources below.

Gale Gale In Context: Opposing Viewpoints

The strongest starting point for this section. Dedicated topic pages on genetic testing, prenatal screening, and disability rights align directly with the rubric's ethical prompts. Combines opinion pieces, news, and primary sources in a single view.

Infobase Issues & Controversies — Infobase

Well-structured pro/con format. Useful if you are having trouble identifying the specific ethical tension in your topic rather than just its general subject. Each entry names the competing claims clearly.

EBSCO Points of View Reference Center — EBSCO

Pairs point/counterpoint essays with background articles and statistics. Models the analytical structure your ethics section should aim for — identifying a position, supporting it with evidence, and engaging seriously with the opposing view.

Advocacy & Professional Resources

Patient Advocacy NORD — National Organization for Rare Disorders

Covers patient advocacy, family impact, accessibility, and community resources. A useful counterpoint to purely clinical perspectives — centers the lived experience of individuals and families affected.

Professional Organization National Society of Genetic Counselors

Public-facing resources from the professional organization for genetic counselors. Essential if your ethical focus is prenatal screening, reproductive decision-making, or counseling practice.

Gale Gale eBooks

Broad eBook reference collection. Useful for ethical frameworks and social science perspectives that add depth beyond the clinical literature.

Background Mayo Clinic

Reliable for general context on patient experience and quality of life. Best used for background understanding rather than as a primary citation.

Not a peer-reviewed source — use for context only.

Guiding Questions for Ethical Analysis

  • What are the benefits and risks of prenatal screening for this condition? Who bears the authority to act on that information?
  • How does the availability of a genetic diagnosis change the experience of families and individuals affected by this disorder?
  • What assumptions do medical and social systems make about people with chromosomal differences, and how do those assumptions affect access to care?
  • How is this condition represented in public media, and does that representation reflect the perspectives of those who actually live with it?
  • What does "quality of life" mean in the context of this disorder? Who is doing the defining?
  • How does genetic counseling balance providing complete information with respecting patient autonomy in reproductive decisions?

Research Skills

Lateral Reading: How to Verify a Source

Lateral reading means leaving a source to look up information about it — rather than reading the source itself more carefully. Professional fact-checkers and researchers use this technique to assess credibility quickly. It is especially important for health and science information, where authoritative-looking sites can present accurate information selectively — or inaccurately.

  1. Open a new tab before you read deeply

    When you encounter an unfamiliar source, resist the impulse to read it thoroughly first. You'll evaluate it more accurately by approaching it from the outside.

  2. Search the publisher or organization

    Who produces this site or journal? Search the organization's name independently. Are they recognized by researchers, libraries, or government agencies? Are they affiliated with a university, hospital, or established institution?

  3. Check what independent sources say about it

    What do other researchers, fact-checkers, or librarians say about this publication or organization? Absence of any recognition can itself be informative.

  4. Look for funding and conflicts of interest

    Is the source funded by an organization with a stake in a particular conclusion? Advocacy groups, pharmaceutical companies, and commercial health sites may present accurate information selectively.

  5. Ask: Is this the original source?

    Many websites summarize research without linking to it. If a site cites a study, locate that study directly. The original research often contains more nuance than the summary suggests — and sometimes says something different entirely.
